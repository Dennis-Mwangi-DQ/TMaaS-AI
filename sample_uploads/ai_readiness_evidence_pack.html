AI Readiness Evidence Pack

Organization: Meridian Retail Group

Sector: Retail and distribution

Prepared for: DQ AI Readiness Assessment

Date: 15 June 2026

Purpose: This document summarizes current data, systems, use-case, people, and leadership conditions for Meridian Retail Group. It is intentionally specific enough for an AI readiness agent to extract evidence and identify contradictions.

1. Executive Summary

Meridian Retail Group operates 18 retail branches and one central warehouse. Leadership wants to use AI to reduce stock-outs, improve demand forecasting, and prioritize customer retention campaigns.

The executive team describes the business as having a "single source of truth" because sales, inventory, and customer records are exported nightly into a cloud warehouse. However, several source systems still use different customer and product identifiers, and reconciliation is performed manually every Monday by the analytics team.

The strongest near-term use case is demand forecasting by branch and product category. The value target is a 15 percent reduction in stock-outs and a 10 percent reduction in slow-moving stock within two quarters.

2. Data Accessibility

Sales transactions from POS, inventory balances from ERP, campaign interactions from CRM, and e-commerce orders are copied into the cloud warehouse every night at 02:00. Finance data is available in the warehouse only as month-end exports.

Store managers cannot query the warehouse directly. They receive Excel summaries from the analytics team. Analysts access the warehouse through SQL and Power BI.

Access permissions are defined by department, but there is no data catalog that explains ownership, refresh schedules, or field definitions across all systems.

3. Data Quality History

Dataset Current Quality Evidence Known Issue
Customer master CRM contains 420,000 customer profiles. About 18 percent of profiles have missing mobile numbers; duplicate customer records are estimated at 11 percent.
Product catalog ERP contains SKU, category, supplier, and unit cost fields. Product category labels differ between ERP and e-commerce for 27 percent of active SKUs.
Sales transactions POS transaction feed is loaded nightly with less than 1 percent missing transaction IDs. Refunds and exchanges are not consistently linked to original transactions.
Inventory movement Warehouse inventory movement is captured daily. Branch-level cycle count adjustments are entered manually and often arrive three to five days late.

There is a documented data quality dashboard for sales and inventory, but not for customer or campaign data. Data quality incidents are discussed informally in weekly operations calls; there is no formal issue log with owners and due dates.

4. Systems Integration

The POS and ERP systems exchange inventory and sales data through scheduled API jobs. CRM and e-commerce data are exported as CSV files and loaded by an orchestration script. HR and training records are not integrated with the analytics environment.

There is no master data management process. Product identifiers are mostly aligned between ERP and POS, but customer identifiers differ across CRM, e-commerce, and loyalty systems.

Contradiction to test: Leadership says the organization has a single source of truth, but customer identifiers differ across three systems and manual reconciliation is still required.

5. Use-Case Specificity

The priority use case is branch-level demand forecasting for the top 2,000 SKUs. The first model should predict weekly demand by branch, SKU, promotion status, and seasonality. The intended decision is replenishment quantity recommendations for branch managers.

Business owners have defined desired outcomes: reduce stock-outs by 15 percent, reduce slow-moving stock by 10 percent, and improve promotion planning accuracy. A baseline forecast accuracy metric has not yet been agreed.

A secondary use case is customer churn prediction for loyalty members. This is less ready because campaign response data is incomplete and consent fields are not consistently captured.

6. Implementation Capability

The analytics team has three BI analysts, one data engineer, and one cloud administrator. There is no in-house machine learning engineer. The team has built Power BI dashboards and SQL data marts, but has not deployed a production predictive model.

IT can support API integrations and cloud storage. Model monitoring, feature engineering, MLOps, and model governance would require external support or new hiring.

The current delivery model is project-based. Business users submit dashboard requests through email, and prioritization happens monthly with the CFO and Head of Operations.

7. Adoption Conditions

Store managers are comfortable using Excel-based stock reports, but they do not currently trust automated replenishment suggestions. The operations team wants recommendations to include a confidence score and a short explanation before managers are asked to act on them.

No formal change management or training plan exists for AI-enabled forecasting. The Head of Operations has agreed to run a six-branch pilot if the tool can be embedded in the existing weekly stock review process.

Warehouse planners are concerned that an AI forecast could be blamed for stock shortages if accountability is not clarified.

8. Leadership Sponsorship

The CFO is the executive sponsor and has approved an exploratory budget of QAR 550,000 for a 12-week discovery and pilot phase. The Head of Operations is the business owner for the demand forecasting use case.

A steering committee meets monthly, but no product owner has been assigned. Legal and compliance have not yet reviewed data privacy implications for customer churn modeling.

The CFO wants a go/no-go decision after the pilot based on measurable business impact, adoption by branch managers, and the reliability of the forecast outputs.

10. Recommended First Action

Before building a model, Meridian should run a two-week data readiness sprint focused on SKU and customer identifier alignment, baseline forecast accuracy, and ownership of data quality issues. The sprint should produce a pilot data dictionary, a cleaned sample dataset for the top 2,000 SKUs, and a named product owner for the forecasting pilot.
